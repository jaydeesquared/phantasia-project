🍎 I Didn't Know My Brain Was Different Until I Was an Adult

🌊 JD · Hyperphantasia · January 2025 · 6 min read

It was one of those nothing evenings. We'd run out of things to watch and were just kind of sitting there, and for some reason I decided to ask my partner to close their eyes and picture an apple. I don't know what I was expecting - maybe I wanted to know if they saw the same slightly battered red one I always get, the one with the specific dent near the stem that I have absolutely no explanation for.

"What apple?" they said.

I laughed. Obviously they were taking the mick.

They were not taking the mick.

"There's no picture. I know it's an apple, I know it's red, I know it sits on a surface - but there's nothing to look at. There's just… the idea of one."

I sat with that. Then kept sitting with it. Then started firing off what I can only describe as increasingly panicked follow-up questions.

What I actually see (apparently not everyone's normal)

When I picture an apple, I see an apple. Not "sort of imagine the concept of" an apple - actually see one, right there, fully formed, with colour and texture and a whole specific quality of light I can't even describe properly. I can rotate it. I can zoom in. If I get distracted I'll end up spending ten minutes mentally following the way light moves across the skin of a piece of fruit I conjured from nothing, and I'll lose the thread of whatever conversation I was meant to be having.

I genuinely had no idea this wasn't standard. "Picture it in your mind" had always been a completely literal instruction to me. I'd heard it in school, in meditation apps, in cooking videos - and every single time I'd just… done it. Seen the thing. Assumed everyone else was doing exactly the same.

The hour that followed

We went properly down the rabbit hole. I kept naming things, my partner kept telling me there was no image. Their childhood bedroom - nothing. Our first holiday together - they remembered it happened, how it felt, what we did, but couldn't picture a single moment of it. Not even a vague impression. Just knowledge, floating around with no visual attached to it.

Meanwhile I'm sitting there realising I've been walking around my whole life with a cinema running in my head and had never once thought to mention it, because I assumed it was completely standard-issue human equipment.

We'd been together for years and had been watching completely different shows. We just didn't know it.

The word "aphantasia" came up about half an hour into the phone research spiral. Then "hyperphantasia" - and suddenly a lot of things made sense about me that I'd just quietly filed under personality quirks. Why I can't read anything remotely scary before bed. Why anxious thoughts don't stay abstract - they arrive fully rendered, with rooms and faces and accurate lighting. Why guided meditation has always felt slightly overwhelming rather than relaxing.

The apple genuinely changed how I understood my own brain. Which is a weird sentence to write, but here we are.
